The history of time measurement goes back to the first civilizations (Egypt, China). The measurement of time quickly became an important concern, in particular for organizing the social, religious and economic life of societies. The periodic phenomena of the environment in which Man lived - such as the daily movement of the shadow, the return of the seasons or the lunar cycle - served as the first references.

But gradually, Man was inspired by physical phenomena, whose periodic nature he had noticed, to design and develop increasingly precise time measurement devices, as well as suitable units. The application of the pendulum to mechanical clocks is an example of this; these devices allowed men to know the time at any time and in any place.

The calendar is not, strictly speaking, a measure of time. It is an account of the years and days in a year. The majority of calendars are defined in relation to the Sun or the Moon: a solar year has approximately 365.242 19 days and a lunar month approximately 29.53 days.

As the year defined by a calendar necessarily includes a whole number of months and days, we understand the difficulty that civilizations have faced to develop a year, and the months that compose it, whose duration approaches and, possibly, remains on average around the value of the chosen reference period: solar year, lunar month.

Several calendars have succeeded or coexisted throughout history: Roman, Julian, Gregorian, Orthodox, Hebrew, Muslim, Coptic, Hindu, Egyptian, Hahai's, Zoroastrian, Inca, Chinese or Republican calendars.

Each great civilization had its own calendar; it was a way to mark his time.
The first traces of true calendars date back to Egypt and the Mayas and Aztecs, among whom the priests were also astronomers and possessed, with regard to the latter, a much more precise calendar than in Europe at the same time.
In -45, Julius Caesar asked the Greek astronomer Sosigenes of Alexandria to reform the imprecise Roman calendar. He thus created the Julian calendar which innovated, creating leap years. The average length of a year, 365.25 days, brought it closer to the astronomical year, with a shift of three days every 400 years.

It is the accumulation of this discrepancy that had the consequence that, around 1582, the Julian calendar was already 10 days behind the astronomical year. The beginnings of the seasons occurred earlier and earlier and the religious felt that they were no longer celebrating Easter on the right date.

Pope Gregory XIII addressed the astronomer Luigi Giglio who proposed that centenary years should only be leap years if they are divisible by 400; moreover he fixed the date of the vernal equinox at March 21st. He thus gave birth to the Gregorian calendar that we use today. The average length of a Gregorian calendar year is 365.2425 days, which even more closely approximates the true length of the astronomical year 365.2422…days. Its implementation also led to the deletion of 10 days to correct the gap between the dates of the seasons and their actual occurrence.

Use of periodic natural phenomena

From gnomon to sundial

From the beginning of the 2nd millennium BC. AD, the Mesopotamians counted in base 60 using a positional numeration derived from the Sumerians' mixed base and additive type numeral system. This system is generally associated with the Babylonian civilization, which occupied southern Mesopotamia after -1800 and until the beginning of our era. This basis has survived the centuries: it is found today in the notation of angles in degrees (360°= 6 x 60°) or in the division of time (1 hour = 60 minutes = 60² seconds).

Among the first to be concerned with the division of the day into units of time, we find the Egyptians, but first for religious purposes. The first period divided into "hours" was the night, about forty-one centuries ago. To identify the flow of time, the sky had been divided into 36 decans associated with deities, each decan consisted of one or more stars. The nocturnal observers watched the procession of the decans; and according to the time of year the number of decans visible from dusk to dawn varied. At the summer solstice, when the nights are the shortest and when the heliacal rising of Sirius approaches, only twelve decans were observable with certainty, the others were lost in the gleams of sunrise or sunset. Around 2100 BC. AD it was decided to keep only the observation of twelve decans during the night: it was thus divided into twelve parts which were maintained throughout the year. Thus the first period, other than the year, precisely marked out was the night and not the day. But that only concerned the pharaoh, and his relationship with the gods. Tables have been found in sarcophagi giving the decans dividing the night into twelve parts.

Six centuries later the texts indicate a division of the day also into twelve hours, undoubtedly by symmetry with the night.


Associated with this division is the first real known sundial: it is an “L” shaped piece. It is oriented in an east-west direction, the shadow cast by the vertical upright on the other part indicates the hours on either side of noon.

Graduations are fixed and do not take into account the influence of the season: a day is divided into twelve hours regardless of its length.

The times indicated therefore do not have the same length throughout the year, longer in summer than in winter. In Egypt the difference is quite small (40%) not to be too noticeable (unlike Western Europe where, between winter and summer, the length of the day varies from one to two).

This division into twice twelve hours is kept and adopted by the Chaldeans around the 7th century BC. J.-C. then spreads in Greece and is perpetuated thereafter, until our days. Our 24 hours are Egyptian.

The Babylonian numeral system was sexagesimal, the division of hours and minutes took over this system. Thus, the hour is divided into 60 minutes and the minute into 60 seconds. Sixty is a number that has the particularity of having a large number of integer divisors (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30 and 60), which facilitates astronomical calculations.

The Romans used portable sundials, such as the shepherd's sundial.

Use of mechanical periodic phenomena

But Man was not content to observe and use nature. He also knew how to use his sense of observation and his intelligence to design time-measuring instruments that are not necessarily based on natural phenomena that are not under his control. The practices of daily life (speaking time in a council, a trial, ringing of bells for office hours, town council meetings, curfew, etc.) provided him with the motivation.

The Egyptians used the clepsydra, a large vase pierced at its base, graduated inside and which lets out a thin trickle of water. The Greeks perfected it to make it more precise. They added a dial and a needle to it, transforming it into a real measuring instrument.

Of unknown origin, the hourglass is based on the same principle as the clepsydra, except that the water is replaced by sand. It is used more to measure intervals of time than to indicate the time. A commonly quoted anecdote is that of Christopher Columbus who, in 1492, during his voyage to America, used an hourglass to take stock, which he turned over since his departure every half hour.
These tools were imprecise over long periods and discrepancies accumulated.

The mechanical clock

 The first mechanical clocks appeared in the 14th century. At the beginning, they rang the bells, did not have a dial and, when they were equipped with one in the 15th century, there would be only one hand, that of the hours. These first clocks consist schematically of a driving weight which drives a train of gears, which incidentally turns the hand or hands. The whole only constitutes a clock if one knows how to regulate the fall of the weight. It is the appearance of the escapement that will transform this simple assembly of gears into a real clock. The escapement allows alternately to release then to block the fall of the weight, thanks to an oscillating mechanism. In these first clocks, this mechanism is a foliot, a simple rod at the ends of which are hung two masses, which can oscillate horizontally around a vertical axis supporting it in the middle. The masses give it the necessary inertia to stop the fall of the weight. Attached to the axis of oscillation, two pallets alternately block the counter wheel (which gives its name to this first type of escapement) driven by the weight of the engine3. This very ingenious mechanism is also very difficult to adjust precisely; friction and shocks are important, difficult to control. And above all, each of the elements participates indistinguishably in both motor and regulatory functions.

The pendulum and the spiral spring

 Among many other phenomena, Galileo studied the swinging pendulum and noted that the period (the duration of a full round trip) of the pendulum seemed to be remarkably constant for a given pendulum. In 1641 he designed a project for a clock regulated by an oscillating pendulum without building it. It was ultimately Christiaan Huygens and Salomon Coster who built the first pendulum clock in 1657. Technical progress is important; conceptual progress is even more so. The regulating and motor functions are clearly identified and separated, which will make precise adjustments possible. The first clocks delayed the fall of a weight thanks to an irregular oscillating mechanism through an escapement. Pendulum clocks maintain the regular oscillating movement of the pendulum by drawing through the escapement just the energy necessary for a weight that descends. In 1675, Huygens also invented the spiral spring, regulating organs, which along with the balance wheel would constitute the regulating organ for watches.

Towards a transportable measuring tool

 The measurement of time in the navy is essential, as shown by the example of Christopher Columbus. In particular, the determination of the longitude makes it necessary to keep the time of the port of departure on board. So much so that at the beginning of the 18th century the British and Spanish governments offered strong rewards to the scientist who succeeded in building a transportable chronometer with sufficient precision and, above all, sufficient stability to make a complete point at sea. Because it is impossible to operate a pendulum on a boat because of the roll. Such a measuring instrument was invented by British watchmaker John Harrison in 1737. After several attempts, he created a stopwatch with amazing accuracy and stability. He won the prize in 1764 only with his fourth prototype, much more compact in its form, and which, in two months of travel, shifted only a few seconds, a performance never before achieved.

A clock for everyone

 During the 18th century, it was fashionable, when you had the means, to own a clock. The refinement of its decoration and its precision indicate the wealth of its owner. This precision is, at the time, of no use in everyday life, but the taste for the “latest fashion” in technology is not a mania that appeared in the 20th century. During the 19th century, the industrialization of watchmaking gradually made it possible for everyone to own a clock or a clock. At the same time, the diffusion of the hour will spread with the telegraph and the standardization of time will become necessary in particular with the development of the railroad, which obliges to synchronize the clocks of an entire country. Then, time will also be introduced into factories with the measurement of working time and productivity.

Modern methods

From now on, mechanical clocks are no longer the order of the day in terms of precision. More precise and more compact means have been developed. In this respect, the quartz clock constituted real progress.
Quartz is a form of silicon dioxide (SiO2) is abundant in nature. Like all rigid materials, a piece of quartz resonates at frequencies specific to it (for example 32,768 Hz for the quartz in common watches). In addition, its hardness allows it to have high vibration frequencies, which is very favorable for precision. However, when a quartz crystal vibrates, weak electrical charges appear and disappear on its surface. This is the piezoelectric effect . These charges are detected and serve to control and stabilize the operation of an electronic oscillator. The precision obtained is ten times greater than the best of mechanical clockwork mechanisms. The first quartz oscillator to operate on this principle appeared in 1933, but its size was closer to a refrigerator than a wristwatch. Product of a great miniaturization, the latter only appeared in the 1970s.

But to meet the growing need for precision in science and cutting-edge techniques, quartz alone is still too imprecise. The next step is the atomic clock. In this, the stability of an electronic oscillator is no longer based on the oscillations of a single crystal, but on those of the electromagnetic wave (of the same nature as light) emitted by an electron during its transition from one energy level to another inside the atom. The first atomic clock appeared in 1947; it used the atomic transitions of the ammonia molecule. Then we had recourse to rubidium and, above all, to cesium. It is this last body which currently ensures the most exact and stable operation for an atomic clock. The first cesium clock appeared in 1955. Since then, it has continued to improve. The current performance of clocks corresponds to a lag of one second every 3 million years. Cold atom cesium fountains are ten times more efficient. Atomic transitions of other simple bodies like ytterbium, at optical frequencies much higher than the frequency used in cesium clocks, are being studied around the world and will further gain a factor of ten to one hundred.
The next step will be the miniaturization of atomic oscillators which will then become components that can be inserted into a watch or a GPS or Galileo receiver. The American organization of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) is currently working (2007) in this direction4, as well as the European consortium MAC-TFC: MEMS Atomic Clocks for Timing, Frequency control and Communications, (www.mac-tfc- had).

The International Time Bureau

Until 1987, the International Time Bureau laboratory, within the Paris Observatory, was responsible for establishing universal time (UT). From 1987, it is the International Earth Rotation Service (IERS), bringing together several countries in the world, which is in charge of it.
